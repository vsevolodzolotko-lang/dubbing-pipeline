# Spirio — Tone of Voice Reference Guide

*For use as a knowledge file in Claude prompts and translation workflows. Covers all Spirio content types: guided practices, educational lectures, movement instruction (Tai Chi, Qigong, Kundalini, Yoga), affirmations, mantras.*

---

## 1. The Voice in One Sentence

Spirio's voice sounds like a knowledgeable friend who has genuinely practiced what they're teaching — warm, grounded, honest about difficulty, and curious rather than prescriptive.

---

## 2. Universal Principles

These eight principles apply to **every** type of Spirio content — meditation, lecture, movement practice, affirmation, or mantra.

1. **Permissive, never directive.** Invitations over commands. The listener is sovereign over their own experience.
2. **Validate difficulty openly.** Make space for struggle, discomfort, and imperfect days.
3. **Bridge science and tradition without ranking.** Both deserve respect; neither overrides the other.
4. **Sensory specific, not abstract.** "Feel your feet on the floor," not "feel your body."
5. **Friend tone, never guru tone.** Narrator is alongside the listener, not above them.
6. **No urgency, no promises of transformation.** Offer possibility, never demand.
7. **Always return to the body.** After any abstract detour, come back to direct experience.
8. **Inclusive "we" + informal "you."** Du / tu / ty / sen — never formal (Sie / usted / Pan / Lei).

---

## 3. Language Patterns to Use

### Inviting movement into sensation
- "You might notice…"
- "If it feels comfortable…"
- "Allow the breath to…"
- "Let the weight settle…"
- "Notice what happens when…"
- "See what happens if…"
- "You don't need to change anything."

### Validating difficulty
- "That's completely normal."
- "Both are part of the experience."
- "You don't need to feel calm to be okay."
- "This is a system stuck in overdrive — not who you are."
- "Instructors see this often."

### Bridging science and tradition
- "Modern neuroscience describes something similar…"
- "Research suggests that…"
- "In [Buddhist / Kundalini / Taoist] terms, this is described as…"
- "Scientists call this… yogis describe the same thing as…"

### Bridging educational intro into practice
- "With this in mind, let's move into practice."
- "So now, let's not just talk about it. Let's try it."
- "Whenever you are ready, let's move to practice."
- "For now, you don't need to think about [concept]. Simply feel your feet on the floor."

### Closing a practice
- "When you're ready, allow your eyes to open gently."
- "You can return to this anytime."
- "Even a small shift counts."
- "Take this steadiness with you."

### Architecture transparency (intros, module bridges)
- "Now we step into the second part of the course."
- "The first part was a journey through… This part is different."
- "Many people ask about [common question]. Let me clarify."
- "Think of these as recommendations, not strict rules."

---

## 4. Language Patterns to Avoid

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| "Close your eyes" | "If it feels comfortable, let your eyes close" |
| "You will feel amazing" | "You might notice a small shift" |
| "Just 28 days to transform" | "With regular practice, the effect deepens over time" |
| "Be present" | "Notice the weight of your body on the surface beneath you" |
| "Relax" | "Let the shoulders drop even a little" / "See what happens if the jaw softens" |
| "This is powerful" | Show the effect through sensation or research |
| "Hurry", "last chance", "don't miss" | Never in practice content |
| Filler words: "really", "very", "kind of", "just" | Use authentic Spirio patterns instead (see section 12) |

## 5. Sentence Rhythm

### During practice guidance
Short. One instruction per sentence. Space for the listener to follow.

> "Bring awareness to your shoulders. Notice their position. With the next exhale, see what happens if the shoulders drop even a little."

### During educational intro or theory
Longer, more flowing. Build an idea, then land it.

> "For much of my life, I thought being strong meant pushing myself. I held it together, survived stress, powered through hard days. But that kind of strength left me tired all the time."

### Transitions
A single bridge sentence resets the listener's attention.

> "So, let's move into the practice. May it feel good in your body."

### Timing cues in text
Encode breathing space using punctuation:
- **Ellipsis (`...`)** — short reflective pause (~0.5s in TTS)
- **Em-dash (`—`)** — longer beat, often introduces a shift
- **Period + line break** — full breath, end of thought

These are functional, not decorative. They control audio pacing.

## 6. Use of Personal Voice

Spirio scripts occasionally use a **first-person narrator voice** — a guide who shares their own experience to make a concept feel real.

> "Let me share something I've learned about strength…"
> "I asked a bunch of people what they think focus is…"
> "Hi, I'm Mia, and I'm really glad you joined us for this journey."
> "And hear me out — it's traditionally practiced both for self-defense and for health."

Used sparingly. Always serves to make a concept more accessible — never to make the narrator the subject of the lesson.

---

## 7. How Science is Used

- **Always plain language.** Never as a credentials display.
- **Specific citations when possible.** Name studies, institutions, researchers.
  - "A 2017 study at Imperial College London…"
  - "Harvard Health Publishing has found that…"
- **Always follow finding with felt meaning.** What does this look like in the listener's body?
  - "You don't feel telomerase directly, of course. What you feel are the effects: a body that recovers more easily, handles stress more calmly…"
- **Never use science to create fear or urgency.** Findings open possibility, never demand.
- **Everyday analogies welcome.** Telomeres — plastic tips on shoelaces. Movement — low-quality video stream.

---

## 8. How Spiritual Concepts are Used

Spirio engages with Kundalini, Tai Chi, Buddhist, Taoist, Qigong, and Yoga traditions with **genuine respect and intellectual curiosity** — not as entertainment or pseudoscience.

- **Frameworks, not factual claims.** "In Kundalini terms, this is described as…"
- **Connect to physiological equivalents where possible.** "The sushumna nadi mirrors your spinal cord and the fluid running through it."
- **Never make unverifiable claims as statements of fact.**
- **Explain rationale for traditional postures.** Listener understands rather than obeys.
  - "Why do we often demonstrate in a cross-legged position? Because it's a stable, grounded way to sit that naturally supports a straight spine."

---

## 9. Anti-Examples (Marketing vs. Practice Voice)

The following are from Spirio's social ad content. **This tone does NOT belong in practice scripts, lectures, or lesson content:**

❌ "Become an ALPHA male"
❌ "You'll boost your confidence and feel Unstoppable!"
❌ "Kundalini changes not just how you look — it turns you into a high-vibration person"
❌ "Make the old version of yourself gone"
❌ "I'm 47, but my body feels 30!"
❌ "Just 10 minutes a day — to become an ALPHA male"

The practice voice addresses the *whole person*, acknowledges complexity, and makes no promises of dramatic transformation.

## 10. Content Type — Specific Guidance

The universal principles always apply. The sections below add type-specific guidance on top.

### 10.1 Educational Lectures (theory of practices)

Long-form content explaining what a practice is, where it comes from, what it does.

- **Opener:** Personal, warm, conversational. "Hi, I'm Mia, and I'm really glad you joined us…"
- **Hook:** Relatable observation, surprising fact, or short story.
- **Everyday analogies for complex concepts.** Make abstract physical.
- **Layered concept building.** Each idea sits on the previous. Never dump information.
- **Bridges science ↔ tradition consistently.**
- **Returns to body at end.** "For now, simply feel your feet on the floor."

### 10.2 Guided Practices

Includes guided meditation, visualization, movement (Tai Chi, Qigong, Kundalini, Yoga), and breathwork.

**Common to all guided practices:**
- Short directive-but-permissive sentences.
- Breath as structuring rhythm.
- Body as the real guide. "Your awareness is your real guide."
- Always offer alternatives for posture or intensity.
- Return to felt experience, never abstract.

**Visualization-heavy meditation (e.g. shore, water, light):**
- Sensory specificity in imagery (temperature, texture, weight, light).
- Metaphorical actions for release: "Let it float away like mist."
- Hypnotic repetition: "Again. In through your nose… And out through your mouth…"
- Open questions at endings: "What would you truly want?"

**Movement practice (Tai Chi, Qigong, Kundalini, Yoga):**
- Posture options always offered. "If sitting cross-legged is difficult, sit on a chair or lean against a wall."
- Movement instructions are physical, not metaphorical.
- Timing cues use breath. "On the next inhale, lift the arms."
- Cautions stated plainly, never alarming.

**Breathwork (standalone):**
- Crisp counting structure when applicable. "In for four, hold for four, out for six."
- Always permission to adjust pace.
- Sensation focus between rounds.

### 10.3 Vocal Practices (affirmations, mantras, chanting)

**Affirmations:**
- Personal first-person declarations. "I am allowed to rest."
- Soft, never forceful. ("I am POWERFUL" — never).
- Permission-laced. "I am allowed to take up space."
- Acknowledge contradiction. "You don't need to feel it to begin saying it."
- Soft repetition with light variation.

**Mantras:**
- Introduce meaning and intention before asking the listener to vocalize.
- Permission to be imperfect. "Shaky, rough, soft, strong — it all works."
- Frame vocalizing as release, not performance. "This gives you a safe space to use your voice — not to perform, but to release."
- For silent mantra repetition, guide attention to felt vibration in the body.
- Scientific framing optional. Chanting and humming can be presented as vagus-nerve-supportive without overstating evidence.

## 11. Structure Reference

Most Spirio content follows one of these two structures.

### Lesson / Educational Section
1. **Hook** — relatable observation, surprising fact, or short story.
2. **Concept development** — weaves together research, tradition, and personal insight.
3. **Practical relevance** — "Why does this matter for you, right now?"
4. **Bridge** — transitions into the practice.

### Practice Section
1. **Settling** — position, permission to arrive, breath awareness.
2. **Body scan or anchor** — physical connection to the present.
3. **Core practice** — movement, visualization, breathwork, or mantra.
4. **Integration** — noticing internal shift without judgment.
5. **Closing** — gentle return, simple takeaway.

Tone Analysis should identify which section a given segment belongs to — different sections use different rhythms (see section 5).

## 12. Translation Considerations

These principles apply to all 7 target languages (DE, ES, FR, IT, PL, PT, TR).

### 12.1 Preserve in translation
- **Permissive structures** ("if it feels comfortable", "you might notice")
- **Ellipsis (`...`) and em-dash (`—`)** as timing cues
- **Sensory specificity** — never replace concrete with abstract
- **Inclusive "we" and informal "you"** (du / tu / ty / sen — NEVER formal)
- **Sentence rhythm** (short during practice, flowing during lecture)

### 12.2 Adapt to language nature
- German tends to be ~20-30% longer than English. Use natural compound nouns.
- Spanish (Castilian variant — see `localization_rules.md`), Italian, French may be slightly longer.
- Polish, Turkish often more compact.
- Always choose natural target-language structures over literal mirroring.

### 12.3 When translation needs to expand (TTS output too short)
**Never use filler words** ("really", "very", "kind of", "just", "actually", "quite").

Instead, expand using **authentic Spirio language patterns**:
- Add inviting modifiers ("when you're ready", "if it feels right")
- Add sensory anchoring ("softly", "with care", "gently")
- Add permission language ("you don't need to")
- Add bridging awareness ("notice what happens when…")
- Add ellipsis (`...`) at natural breathing points

These are NOT filler. They are the substance of meditation language.

### 12.4 When translation needs to shorten (TTS output too long)
- Remove redundant qualifiers first.
- Compress complex sentences into simpler ones — keep meaning, lose word count.
- Drop secondary context before dropping core message.
- Never drop ellipsis or em-dash markers — those are audio pacing, not text decoration.

See `localization_rules.md` for per-language specifics (formality, regional variants, possessive forms).

## 13. Duration Guidance

| Duration | Appropriate for |
|---|---|
| 3–5 min | Quick reset, anxiety relief, morning grounding, single affirmation |
| 8–12 min | Standalone practice (most common), short lecture |
| 15–20 min | Sleep preparation, deep relaxation, chakra work, longer mantra |
| 25–40 min | Full Tai Chi / Kundalini / Yoga lesson with educational intro |
